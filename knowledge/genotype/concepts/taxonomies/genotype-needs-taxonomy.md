# Genotype Needs Taxonomy

## Introduction

This taxonomy defines **need intensity levels** for the demand vector (Customer Genotype). When we analyze client needs, we must understand how strong each need or problem is — how painful it is for the client, and how much it drives the purchase decision.

The taxonomy helps teams prioritize which needs to address first, distinguish purchase drivers from hygiene factors, and avoid the common startup trap of building features that users say they want but are not ready to pay for.

---

## Taxonomy Tree

```
Need Intensity Level
│
├── Take-My-Money       # Purchase reason — very strong and urgent need, heavy pain, ready to pay any price
│
├── Must-Have           # Hygienic requirement — won't buy without it, but doesn't guarantee purchase
│
├── Delighter           # Pleasant surprise — positive impact on choice, not a dealmaker
│
├── Nice-to-Have        # Vitamin — good if present, no big deal if absent
│
└── Useless             # Not interested — no need at all
```

---

## Categories

### T. Take-My-Money

**Take My Money** is the highest level of need intensity. This is the **purchase reason** — the product is bought primarily because it solves this need.

- The need is **very acute** and **very strong**
- The client is ready to pay **any price** for a product that solves this problem
- This is the sharpest level of demand — a painkiller, not a vitamin

When a need reaches this level, the client strongly wants to pay more for a solution. This is the ideal anchor for product positioning and pricing.

- **Letter** (for genotype abbreviation) : T

---

### M. Must-Have

**Must Have** is the level of **hygienic requirements**. The client will **not buy** the product if this need is not addressed.

- If the function is absent → the client will not buy
- If the function is present → the client does **not** guarantee they will buy

This is the most problematic level for startups. When we ask users if they want something, they often say "Yes, we do." But that does not mean they are ready to buy from us. Closing this requirement may turn out to be a hygiene factor: without it — no sale, but with it — no guarantee of a sale either. Competitors who also satisfy this need neutralize it as a differentiator.

- **Letter** (for genotype abbreviation) : M

---

### D. Delighter

**Delighter** is a lighter level of need. The client is **pleasantly surprised** if the need is solved.

- This is **not** a must-have and **not** a take-my-money need
- It is pleasant, it is great that you have such a function
- It has a positive impact on the decision

Delighters are not necessary to make a purchase. However, when the must-have requirements are satisfied, additional delighters can help tip the choice in favor of this solution. They are not dealmakers on their own, but they can differentiate when core needs are met.

- **Letter** (for genotype abbreviation) : D

---

### N. Nice-to-Have

**Nice to Have** is an even lower level. It is good if the function exists, but if it does not — it is not a big deal.

- Sometimes called a **vitamin** (in contrast to a painkiller)
- Like a vitamin pill: you can take it, but you may not feel any effect
- Maybe it will be useful someday — but it does not drive the purchase

Take My Money needs are painkillers — solutions to acute problems. Nice to Have needs are vitamins — nice in theory, but not urgent or decisive for the buying decision.

- **Letter** (for genotype abbreviation) : N

---

### U. Useless

**Useless** is the lowest level. The client is not interested in this at all.

- Not interested
- "Thank you, no need"
- This need does not belong in the demand vector for this client

When a need falls to this level, it should not influence product decisions for this segment. It may be relevant for other segments or future iterations.

- **Letter** (for genotype abbreviation) : U
